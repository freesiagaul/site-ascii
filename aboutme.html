"How did I get here?""



I am often asked this, especially about my childhood, so for the very curious, here's a tale.
Buckle up.

I was born to two ski bums who decided, “Let’s have a baby and start a business in the same year!” It worked out great, except I ended up moving schools 13 times and was born into an unpaid internship.
(Kidding, sort of.)
Naturally, I didn't like school much Curriculums never matched. Teachers forgot I existed. I once had to learn Chinese... in French. (I don't know French.) I was accelerated, decelerated, put into speech therapy for my accent, then the school would get upset at me missing the classes they were taking me out of. I was too cheeky, too loud, or too argumentative. Always too much. This led to me retreating into my computer. It was the only thing that I had that I knew wouldn't leave when I moved to the next school.
Thus began the best time of my life.
Pokemon, Minecraft, Halo. (The greats) I watched Pokemon over 20 times by 10, sold fully trained Pokemon to people on my school bus for money to then 'invest' into Minecraft roles, had over 4000 hours on Minecraft building colosseum after colosseum, and would sneak out after dark to jump through the window of a similarly lonely person and we would boot up Halo. I was this elusive transfer kid in every homeroom that would never show up to school. I would also sneak out to the garage and Macguyver ziplines or declare that I was going to make a tree house (for the 11th time.) I skipped school a lot after learning if I aced my classes and had a 50% attendance mark I would pass. Finding a few good bathroom windows to jump from. I master the art of being 'sick' on days too snowy to walk home. This meant I had a lot more time to learn things. I started to toy around with the idea of being a programmer. A friend dragged me to her dad’s game-dev class so she wouldn’t be alone. I left with my first 3D model, Penelope the Bunny, and an obsession with Unity fog settings. I got really into developing 3D games for VR, I can still recall meticulously timing and animating each cloud to be just right. By 11-14, I was certain that school was just baby sitting. All of my friends existed in another online world that felt more real to me. I would go into virtual spaces with them, or stay in call for hours as we would play Overwatch or Dungeons and Dragons in VRChat. After playing for three straight days of Overwatch until the TV blacked out and the PS4 started smoking, I looked up at the ceiling and asked,
"What am I doing with my life?
That, compounded with a silly accident where I almost died in the mountains in a blizzard earlier in the year, which made me even more set on not going outside tipped to a breaking point. I was known for my cubby in the closet by this stage. I coded, did art, all of my time was spent on hobbies or games. Eventually, I got tired of moving and declared that I would live with my grandparents in Australia so that I could apply to University without my transcript being a nightmare to any admissions officer.
(That's what I say, but in reality, I wanted to see what it was like to live in one place.)
As my parents planned to wave their good byes and fly back to Canada, the pandemic hit. The pandemic was the best time of my life. Now I was forced not to go to school. How good! I remember spending 18 days in a Discord call with 7 of my online friends, essentially living in VR, OSU, or working on our hobbies. We challenged ourselves to see how long we could stay in it for. At this stage coding as a job was the plan. I liked that you didn't need a degree to prove that you weren't dumb. I liked the culture of freedom. CS50 is just online, loads of MIT courses are just online, you can learn anything! At this stage, I had made one friend at my actual school, and he sent me a quantum computing course ran my MIT and Google over messages and jabbed, "You would do something like this." Spitefully, I applied for a full scholarship. I ended up getting in and only then realised at that point the gravity of it. This was a university style courses for two semesters, taught by some interesting people. It was on every night on the weekend for two semesters (12am-3am, as I live in Australia and it was US-based.) I got a 98% by the end, (2% lost on attendance because I would pass out sometimes.) I really liked it. Then I went on to just start learning more on my free time. I picked up vector calculus, fell in love with Feynman, particle physics, MIT OpenCourseware, editing pure math Wikipedia pages - everything. Everything was magic again. I realised how beautiful the world is, mathematically and philosophically. At this stage I had something called the "Question Book." I would think about something simple. e.g.
"What is colour?"
Then I would run off and research it relentlessly. I had a blog at the time, and I would write to nobody. I called it "On Zero," it was a nod to how everything I learned started from a complete beginning, and something about starting completely on zero is just thrilling. (My favourite topic ended up being light scattering.) The blogs had figures, were written in LaTex and all, and I would just play pretend PhD. I was really into Scott Aaronson's blog at the time, which inspired it.I'd make experiments or just find every book I could on things like solid state physics or make flash cards on shearing effects, how to classify rigid bodies based on their symmetries, or how to represent quantum information. Eventually there wasn't enough details on websites, so I eventually changed from the "Question Book" to printing out research papers in photonic and semiconductor based quantum research and would read them. Whenever I encountered a word I didn't know, I would start learning about it online using textbooks and courses. So If I didn't understand Hamiltonian, I would then run off and challenge myself to take an online course that explained it all, do the tests, then go back to the paper. When I started to get the hang of it, I would read the beginning and try to guess the end based on what I knew.
It was so fun. At this point I was racking up online courses like an achievement hunter, so as I applied to more and more quantum courses, eventually one of the admissions ladies (who normally works for Harvard admissions) said I couldn't join because I was too advanced and would be taking someone's spot. I think she must have felt bad or something, because she offered to let me be a teaching assistant alongside their PhD students. This was quite thrilling to a kid who was an okay report card. (It was all A's essentially, but the Pareto Principle greatly inspired my cares toward achieving 100's when I could learn something real instead.) It was good for someone who didn't go to class and didn't know if they wanted to go to university, but it wasn't going to get me into an incredible university either. It started to confirm my theory about learning. At this point I was around 16, and just continued doing more and more courses and textbooks online. Eventually, the Sydney Quantum Academy was hosting their first Quantum Conference and I weaseled my way in. I ended up meeting the guy who designed the "Quantum Engineering" course that I chose later on when we were sat across the table and he asked me what degree I was doing and went "I'm in Year 10." (I immediately got a lab tour.) At this stage I still wanted to be a theoretical physicist, or a real estate agent (whichever one would give me more time and freedom to just do what I like.) At the same time, I was starting to experiment with building physical things. Go karts, hydroelectric funnel prototypes for the Alps, I even wrote up a material and financial report detailing an idea to redesign an area of my high school and pitched it well enough that they actually changed it. A STEM teacher started giving me E-Waste to take apart whenever I was at school, and gave me a drill, winked, and said, "Fetch me the motors." From there, I continued teaching quantum mechanics alongside PhD's, went to UNSW for Quantum Engineering, and was a podium finisher for an MIT hackathon on my first day of university!


I could write and write, but that's most of me before I was 18!

FAQ

Why quantum engineering?

Quantum engineering is this cool intersection between three of my favourite things: electrical engineering, physics, and computers!

It's a degree that promised to never run out of problems. My degree essentially trains me as an electrical engineer with higher expectations, while still emphasising low-level coding abilities and competence with physics. Beyond the core content of my degree, my electives are going to focus on telecommunications and microelectronics.

You don't sound Australian?

That's right! I'm a dual-citizen (Australian-Canadian), I have spent the majority of my life in Australia, but was born to two entrepreneurs who moved to Canada to found a successful skiing business!

I spent much of my life moving back and forth (going to 13 schools!)

Because I moved so much I started to really enjoy playing games on my computer, and eventually a friend invited me to a video-game making class her dad was hosting when I was young, then later on I looked up "CS50."

The rest is history! :)
