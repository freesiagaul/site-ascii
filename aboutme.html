"How did I get here?""



I am often asked this, especially about my childhood, so for the very curious, here's a tale.
I'm an open book - but it's a long and windy book.
Buckle up.

I was born to two ski bums who decided, “Let’s have a baby and start a business in the same year!” It worked out great, except I ended up moving schools 13 times and was born into an unpaid internship.
(Kidding. Sort of. I did end up making product designs and emerging from under conference tables to pitch, from Colorado to Sydney.)
Naturally, I didn't like school much Curriculums never matched. I once had to learn Japanese... in French. (I don't know French.) I was accelerated, decelerated, put into speech therapy for my accent, then reprimanded for missing the classes they were taking me out of. I was too cheeky, too loud, too argumentative. I held my ground when I knew I was right. Teachers didn't like me, despite being the top of the class. The structures were absurd. This led to me retreating into my computer. These people will be replaced by a new cast in 6 months, my computer won't. Thus began the best time of my life.
Pokemon, Minecraft, Halo. (The greats) I watched Pokemon over 20 times by 10, sold fully trained Pokemon to people on my school bus for money to then 'invest' into Minecraft roles, had over 4000 hours on Minecraft building colosseum after colosseum, and would sneak out after dark to jump through the window of a similarly lonely person and we would boot up Halo. I was this elusive transfer kid in every homeroom that would never show up to school. I would also sneak out to the garage and Macguyver ziplines or declare that I was going to make a tree house (for the 11th time.) I skipped school a lot after learning if I aced my classes and had a 50% attendance mark I would pass. Finding a few good bathroom windows to jump from. I mastered the art of being 'sick' on days too snowy to walk home. The walk being 4 kilometers up a mountain, normally in snow.

I started to toy around with the idea of being a programmer. I knew deep down that I would always love travelling and meeting interesting people. The flexibility and the fact that it was like art were huge to me. A friend dragged me to her dad’s game-dev class so she wouldn’t be alone. I left with my first 3D model, Penelope the Bunny, and an obsession with Unity fog settings.

I got really into developing 3D games for VR, I can still recall meticulously timing and animating each cloud to be just right. I remember setting out the map as much as the Grand Canyon in Unreal Engine as I could, and I still have some of the concept art of "Laplaces Lair" that took a physics twist on games similar to Super Smash Bros (I did these when I was a little bit older, around 13.) From 11-14, I was certain that school was baby sitting. All of my friends existed in another online world that felt more real to me. I would go into virtual spaces with them, or stay in call for hours as we would play Overwatch or Dungeons and Dragons in VRChat. After playing for three straight days of Overwatch until the TV blacked out and the PS4 started smoking, I looked up at the ceiling and asked, "What am I doing with my life? I had learned the game, had everything I wanted. I did sports well, I played games for days, I was the debate champion of the region, I was getting community medals, I was learning how to paint from professional painters. I consistently got podium after podium. My dad and sister both were on the national ski team - and I was put into an athlete school. I picked up things fast.

But it still felt hollow.

I needed something harder.

That, compounded with a silly accident where I almost died of hypothermia in a blizzard earlier in the year while trapped on a mountain range, all made me even more set on not going outside for anything that wasn't functional. I was known for my cubby in the closet by this stage. This tipped me to a breaking point. I needed a circuit breaker, I needed a challenge, I needed to feel uncomfortable.

I declared that I would live with my grandparents in Australia so that I could apply to University without my transcript being a nightmare to any admissions officer.
(Which was true.)
As my parents planned to wave their good byes and fly back to Canada, the pandemic hit. Then my dad got diagnosed with stage 4 cancer, so they stayed in the country and sold their business. I remember spending 18 days in a Discord call with 7 of my online friends, essentially living in VR, Minecraft, OSU (one of my friends was top 100 in the world so I made cover art for his videos), or working on hobbies. We challenged ourselves to see how long we could stay in it for. At this stage coding as a job was the plan. I liked that you didn't need a degree to prove that you weren't dumb. I liked the culture of freedom. CS50 is just online, loads of MIT courses are just online, you can learn anything! At this stage, I had made one friend at my actual school, and he sent me a quantum computing course ran my MIT and Google over messages and jabbed, "You would do something like this." Spitefully, I applied for a full scholarship. I ended up getting in and only then realised at that point the gravity of it. This was a university style courses for two semesters, taught by some interesting people. It was on every night on the weekend for two semesters (12am-3am, as I live in Australia and it was US-based.) I got a 98% by the end, (2% lost on attendance because I would pass out sometimes.) I really liked it. Then I went on to just start learning more on my free time. I picked up vector calculus, fell in love with Feynman, particle physics, MIT OpenCourseware, editing pure math Wikipedia pages - everything. Everything was magic again. I realised how beautiful the world is, mathematically and philosophically. At this stage I had something called the "Question Book."
I would think about something simple. e.g.
"What is colour?"
Then I would run off and research it relentlessly. I had a blog at the time, and I would write to nobody. I called it "On Zero," it was a nod to how everything I learned started from a complete beginning, and something about starting completely on zero is just thrilling. This is a screenshot of a part of one of the old blog posts on the right.
(My favourite topic ended up being light scattering. Light is amazing.) I would just play pretend PhD. I was really into Scott Aaronson's blog at the time, which inspired it. I'd make experiments or just find every book I could on things like solid state physics or make flash cards on shearing effects, how to classify rigid bodies based on their symmetries, or how to represent quantum information.

Eventually, there wasn't enough details on websites, so I changed from the "Question Book" to printing out research papers in photonic and semiconductor based quantum research and would read them. Whenever I encountered a word I didn't know, I would start learning about it online using textbooks and courses. So If I didn't understand Hamiltonian, I would then run off and challenge myself to take an online course that explained it all, do the tests, then go back to the paper. When I started to get the hang of it, I would read the beginning and try to guess the end based on what I knew. It was so fun. At this point I was stacking up online courses like an achievement hunter, from astronomy to theoretical topology, I was addicted. I also applied to more and more quantum courses. I gravitated toward quantum because it was highly technical and had a bit of everything. I applied to another course and one of the admissions ladies (who normally works for Harvard admissions) said I couldn't join because I was too advanced and would be taking someone's spot. I think she must have felt bad or something, because she offered to let me be a teaching assistant alongside their PhD students. This was quite thrilling to a kid who was an okay report card. (It was all A's essentially, but the Pareto Principle greatly inspired my cares toward achieving 100's when I could learn something real instead.) It was good for someone who didn't go to class and didn't know if they wanted to go to university, but it wasn't going to get me into an incredible university either. It started to confirm my theory about learning. At this point I was around 16, and just continued doing more and more courses and textbooks online. I found a way to sneak to the edge of the school yard, get behind the sports shed, and run into the forest. I found a route through the forest from my highschool to where I was living. I told no one at my school that I was into quantum mechanics or anything like it. Tall poppy syndrome is very real in regional areas. Eventually, one teacher found out and I told them why I was running home, and they stayed quiet for me. "Our little secret." They were one of the only people that I will forever regard as a teacher. I learned that sharing my interests where I live would only lead to bullying. Broken phones, hair cut, getting chased in public places, computers cracked. So I had this second life. It built up my confidence, suddenly I had a tribe within the comfort of anonymity. Sure, they didn't actually exist where I lived, but I didn't feel so alone anymore.

For my whole life, people in real life would either love me or they would hate me. No in-between treatment. One person, who later became my friend said that, "You just stand out. Like you look like you should be bullied, but you just... do things anyways? I think a lot of people tried to target you for that. "

One day, I dealt with my bullies when the 2 heads of their cult said they could beat me up. I was so done with them, that even if I didn't win, I was jumping at the opportunity to get a good square punch in their faces. (I was representing the school for state athletics at the time and started a new record for shot put distance at the school.)
I said,
2 versus 1, 5:00 pm beach, tonight.

I made sure a crowd of people showed up to see that they were too scared. After that, I gained some relative freedom from judgement until graduation. A bit later on (age 17), some younger girls were actually cutting their hair like me (which feels very strange,) and I ran for school president with a majority vote, but the school leadership voted against me, essentially saying I was, "Too deviant." Beyond this, I became obsessed with ancient Greece and Persia, poetry, and philosophy (my favourites ended up being Popper, Camus, Beauvoir and Bertrand Russel). Real philosophy (not some influencers tips on 'Stoicism')

I ended up making the Kuhn-Popper Debate Wikipedia page after having a long sit down with a teacher over why I thought school was broken, which is how I stumbled upon philosophy.

He said,
"You seem to care a lot more about truth than passing."

This is what the Kuhn-Popper debate is all about. Going against the masses and holding your footing based on deductive facts. A strong conviction that the most moral version of the world is one with open debate. This specific debate was at the turning point of when belief and fear started to matter more than truth in our world.

Back to quantum. I'm 16, and the Sydney Quantum Academy was hosting their first Quantum Conference and I weaseled my way in. I ended up meeting the guy who designed the "Quantum Engineering" course that I chose later on when we were sat across the table and he asked me what degree I was doing and went "I'm in Year 10." (I immediately got a lab tour.)

At this stage I still wanted to be a theoretical physicist, or a real estate agent (whichever one would give me more time and freedom to just do what I like.) At the same time, I was starting to experiment with building physical things. Go karts, swords, hydroelectric funnel prototypes for the Alps, I even wrote up a material and financial report detailing an idea to redesign an area of my high school and pitched it well enough that they actually changed it. A STEM teacher started giving me e-waste to take apart whenever I was at school, and gave me a drill, winked, and said, "Fetch me the motors."
I picked up a hobby of ripping open thrown away devices and either modding them or taking out parts I would find useful in future projects. I also started road tripping during classes - I worked loads of part time jobs to buy things from department stores and pay for gas. I would bring a thermos and a load of ramen, some books on stars, and a telescope. I would sleep out of my car. It was awesome. Probably the funniest things that happened was, I parked my car on a field near a highway once to watch stars for the night, but as mist rolled into the valley, I packed up. Police drove over to the car, curious about what was going on. The guy walked up to me and asked,
What are you doing here?

Me, excited that someone asked me about my hobbies went,
I'm looking at the stars!

I then pointed quickly up at the pitch black sky caused by the mist.
Hey miss, have you had any drugs or alcohol in the last 24 hours?
This was then followed by me frantically trying to backtrack and explain why I needed to be on this exact field to watch what I wanted to watch, ripping telescopes and nebulae books out of my car. The guy totally thought I was bonkers.

This was two days after finishing my final exams, which may give you an insight into what I was thinking at the time.
(I make video time capsules to my future self.)

After a nightmarish year (having to move again and much more. This year, I lived.) I finished high school, then a week after graduating, my dad passed. Dad was the 'crazy evangelical founder' of my parents. We were both absolute nightmares to live with, but it made me reflect on how he changed me in a good way. This was my funeral speech.

FAQ

Why quantum engineering?

Quantum engineering is a degree for the highly technical generalist.

It's a degree that promised to never run out of problems. You're essentially trained as an electrical engineer who knows quantum physics.

You don't sound Australian?

That's right! I'm a dual-citizen (Australian-Canadian), I have spent the majority of my life in Australia, but was born to two entrepreneurs who moved to Canada to found a successful skiing business!

I spent much of my life moving back and forth (going to 13 schools!)

Because I moved so much I started to really enjoy playing games on my computer, and eventually a friend invited me to a video-game making class her dad was hosting when I was young, then later on I looked up "CS50."

The rest is history! :)
